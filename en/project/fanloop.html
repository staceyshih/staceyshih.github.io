FanLoop Trendy Video App

Overview

In 3 months, we designed a new short and long form video platform app from 0 to 1 — conducting business validation research and UI/UX design to support market expansion into short-form video content.

* Currently in soft launch, the app has reached 100+ downloads, offering 500+ hours of video content and collaborating with 50+ brands and influencers.

Goal

Business Goal
CATCHPLAY operates in OTT streaming, film distribution, and production. To maximize business synergy, the company aimed to expand its market presence from a paid long-form video platform to a free short-form video model.

Short-term
Increase exposure and reach for in-house and licensed long-form content while generating ad revenue.

Long-term
Enter the short-form video creator market in Indonesia, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia, attracting high-quality content creators through industry-tailored features and exclusive partnerships.


Design Goal
  1. Designed an app that supports both long and short video viewing, with customizable content redirection features to meet business needs.
  2. Since short-form video was a new domain for the team, we agreed to conduct user research to validate the business concept before development, and optimize designs based on the findings.

Background

With fragmented viewing habits on the rise, we saw an opportunity: short, interruptible videos were gaining traction while long-form engagement declined. We designed a service that supports video "snacking" behavior while driving traffic back to full-length content.

Research

Research Goal

Since the app was initiated by business needs, the research focused on increasing user willingness and validating design assumptions. Research goals:

  1. Understand users' perceptions and motivations for using short video apps.
  2. Identify essential features and prioritize key functions for MVP.
  3. Gather insights to inform design decisions.
Research plans were developed based on these three objectives.

Target Audience

Aged 25–35, divided into two groups:

  1. Existing CATCHPLAY+ users who enjoy its content.
  2. Non-members with short video app experience.

Research Methods

To quickly validate the concept and define MVP scope, we used:

  1. Competitive Analysis

    2. Studied multiple short video apps and built an initial prototype.

  2. 1-on-1 User Interviews

    3. Explored user expectations and tested UI concepts.

  3. Kano Analysis

    4. Identified basic, attractive, and expected features.

Research Process

  1. Explanation of interview objectives
  2. Motivations, habits, and experiences of using short video apps
  3. Introduction & walkthrough of the FanLoop App concept
  4. KANO Research questionnaire, along with brief interviews
  5. Overall feedback sharing

Due to time constraints and to maximize the number of interviewees, we chose to conduct remote interviews. In addition to presenting slides, we also provided a clickable prototype link, allowing users to interact while clarifying any confusion. This helped users better understand the concept during the research process, enabling more accurate responses and insights, and improving the effectiveness of concept validation.

To help users better engage with the context, the video content in the prototype was optimized based on trending topics, in collaboration with the editorial team.

Insights

1. Motivation for watching short videos: “Wasting time meaningfully”
Users tend to be passive in choosing content; too many options cause decision fatigue. 80% of respondents preferred to start watching directly for a more immersive experience, simply skipping what they don’t like.
2. Short videos as “indexes” — not substitutes for long-form videos, but part of the filtering process
Users highly value time management, even during video consumption. They often filter for “important segments” using playback speed or skipping irrelevant parts. They prefer to preview with short videos before deciding to watch the full version.
3. Content strategy plays a key role
“Exclusive content” is the main download incentive, while “social interaction” is not critical since users already have their go-to platforms. Interestingly, users showed high regard for original copyright: some mentioned downloading TikTok after seeing clips reposted on Instagram to support the original creators or watch full content.
4. Features related to “watching videos” should be prioritized
Many video features are expected basics — such as saving, playback speed control, timeline, and preview thumbnails. Poor implementation may erode user trust. Other features like watching without registration or external links are attractive but non-essential. Most users don’t need to create their own videos; they primarily seek high-quality content here.
Overall, the two user groups showed little difference in feature expectations. Initially, we were concerned that existing CP users might dislike or be unfamiliar with short videos, but preferences across new and experienced users were similar — indicating that prior short video experience does not strongly affect app acceptance.

Design Strategies

Based on our research, we developed the following design strategies:

1. Instantly enter viewing mode to reduce choice anxiety

The homepage presents a large-format mix of long and short videos, which start playing automatically when in view.

2. Intuitive gestures to navigate related content

To enable meaningful exploration, users can control the depth and breadth of content discovery instead of encountering fully random content.
Inspired by common swipe gestures, we mapped directions to different content relationships:

  • Up/Down: Random recommendations based on watch history
  • Left/Right: Switch to related content via hashtags

3. There’s always a next step

Browsing doesn’t end after a video finishes. We offer customizable external links to drive traffic — such as full-length video platforms, official websites, and media articles. Users can also explore creator channel pages or click hashtags for more related content.

Outcome

This research influenced not only UI design but also content strategy and business decisions. The main contributions to the product team include:

  1. Helping the team focus MVP development and shorten decision-making time

    2. Due to limited development resources, we prioritized only the most essential features. The research helped the team reach MVP consensus quickly.

  2. Optimizing UI design strategy to better align with user habits

    3. As noted, we focused on improving video viewing and gesture interactions to ensure the product is intuitive upon launch.

  3. Using prototyping to boost workflow efficiency, cutting discussion time by 30%

    4. As the concept was new and unfamiliar, the short video prototypes were reused for presentations, cross-team discussions, and collaboration proposals with editors and external content providers.

Learning & Next Step

Consider the real needs of both end-users and content creators

In unfamiliar domains, we need to empathize not only with target users but also with content creators and internal content teams. Will our design increase maintenance costs? Is it necessary?

Product validation still has limitations

Whether existing users would adopt this product remains uncertain. However, research helped identify “recognizable product value” and ways to optimize current design to meet user needs.

Remote interviews made it difficult to observe user interaction and offer guidance, which affected prototype effectiveness. Pilot tests and timely updates to research materials helped improve accuracy and efficiency.

One issue we faced: the feature prioritization in KANO interviews didn’t convince all stakeholders. Later reviews revealed researchers must align feature candidates and phrasing with all “decision-making stakeholders” beforehand to ensure research results influence development order.
